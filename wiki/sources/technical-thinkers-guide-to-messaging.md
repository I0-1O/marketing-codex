# The Technical Thinker's Guide to Messaging That Moves People

> Full text: [[technical-thinkers-guide-to-messaging]] (articles/)

## Synopsis

Written for technical people in or alongside product marketing — PMs, technical PMMs, marketers from engineering backgrounds. The thesis: the best messaging combines structural rigor with genuine customer empathy. Neither alone is enough.

The technical thinker's default (decomposition, feature organization) produces accurate, comprehensive artifacts that ask buyers to do the translation work themselves. The empathy shift means starting from the customer's frustration — writing pain points in the customer's voice, not the product team's language. Structure then earns its keep by turning that empathy into a reusable, scalable messaging architecture.

The practical centerpiece is the [[master-messaging-document]] framework: three pillars, each named for a customer problem cluster, with a defined internal sequence (pain points → use cases → features/differentiators → value proposition → outcomes). The forest-and-trees diagnostic: can you explain the entire positioning in three sentences without referencing a feature? If not, the doc has drifted into the trees.

## Key Arguments

- The audience rewards relevance, not thoroughness. Relevance starts with their problem, not your product.
- Pain point test: do they sound like a customer talking about their day, or a product team describing what they built?
- Problem-first messaging gives [[matthew-dixon-brent-adamson]] Challenger reps the raw material to teach and reframe — pain points = opening, feature transition = reframe, value prop = the insight
- PM-PMM collaboration: PMs bring depth (what was built and why), PMMs bring framing. Most valuable PM contribution isn't a feature list — it's verbatim customer quotes
- Structure without empathy = accurate and ignorable. Empathy without structure = resonates once, can't be replicated.
