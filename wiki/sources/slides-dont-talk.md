# Slides Don't Talk

> Full text: [[slides-dont-talk]] (articles/)

## Synopsis

A presentation is a story told by a person. Slides support the story — they do not tell it. This article diagnoses why most decks fail (three bad habits: teleprompter slide, thoroughness-as-clarity, leave-behind fallacy), argues the leave-behind constraint no longer exists in a world of recordings and AI transcripts, and provides a structural framework for building decks that require a presenter to work.

The central tests: Can this deck be understood without anyone presenting it? (If yes, it's a document.) Does every piece of text answer "does the audience need to *see* this?" (If not, it belongs in speaker notes, not on the slide.)

Key references: [[garr-reynolds]] (*Presentation Zen*, "slideument"), [[edward-tufte]] (*Cognitive Style of PowerPoint*), [[nancy-duarte]] (*Resonate*), [[barbara-minto]] (Pyramid Principle).

## Key Arguments

- The leave-behind fallacy is institutional memory from a pre-recording world — the constraint it solved no longer exists
- Slides compete with the presenter when they contain things the audience needs to *hear* rather than *see* (Mayer: on-screen text + narration hurts comprehension)
- Speaker notes are the presentation; slides are the punctuation
- The Pyramid Principle is for thinking, not presenting — literal application eliminates narrative tension
- Steve Jobs example: slides so simple they could not compete with him; the story belonged to him entirely
