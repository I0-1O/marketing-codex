# Battle Card

## Definition

A one-page (or short-form) sales reference document that equips reps to win deals against a specific named competitor. Designed for use in live sales conversations — not for PMM research or strategy.

## Why It Matters

Reps encounter competitive objections in-call and don't have time to read a competitive brief. A battle card is the distilled, actionable version: what to say, what not to say, and how to reframe the conversation. Without it, reps default to feature comparisons — which is the worst possible frame for a price-sensitive buyer.

## How It Works

A battle card typically covers:

- **Competitor snapshot** — who they are, their positioning, their typical buyer
- **When you win / when they win** — honest assessment of deal scenarios
- **Your differentiated strengths** — specific to this competitor, not generic
- **Their weaknesses** — substantiated, not wishful thinking
- **Landmines** — things the competitor will say about you; anticipated attacks
- **Trap-setting questions** — questions reps can ask to expose competitor weaknesses
- **Objection responses** — specific rebuttals with proof points

## Battle Card vs. Competitive Brief

These are different artifacts for different audiences:

| | Battle Card | Competitive Brief |
|---|---|---|
| Audience | Sales rep in a live deal | PMM, product, leadership |
| Length | 1 page | 4–10 pages |
| Purpose | Win the conversation | Understand the landscape |
| Depth | Actionable, curated | Comprehensive |
| Update cadence | High (every major release) | Moderate |

Conflating the two produces documents that are too long for reps to use and too shallow for PMM to trust.

## Update Cadence

Standard advice is quarterly. Doherty ([[30-minute-battlecard-doherty]]) argues for **continuous intelligence** — update on competitive trigger events (competitor announcement, lost deal pattern, pricing change) rather than calendar. The 30-minute AI-accelerated workflow makes this practical where an 8-hour manual process didn't.
