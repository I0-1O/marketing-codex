# Problem-First Messaging

## Definition

A messaging discipline that starts from the customer's frustration before mentioning the product. The structural commitment: pain points come first, capabilities come second. Empathy is not soft — it's a testable, disciplinable skill that produces materially different messaging output.

## Why It Matters

Technical thinkers and product-proximate marketers default to capability-first messaging: organize the features, map them to outcomes, present the architecture. These documents are accurate and comprehensive. They fail because they ask the buyer to do the translation work — "here are our capabilities, you figure out why they matter to you." Buyers won't. They'll scan it, decide it looks like every other vendor's material, and move on.

The audience doesn't reward thoroughness. They reward relevance. Relevance starts with their problem.

When a sales rep opens a deck organized around customer problems, they're leading with empathy. When they open one organized around product capabilities, they're leading with a pitch. Buyers feel the difference in the first thirty seconds.

## The Pain Point Voice Test

Open your messaging document. Read only the pain points.

Do they sound like a **customer talking about their day**? Or a **product team describing what they built**?

**Product team voice:** "We need advanced exception handling for complex workflow scenarios."

**Customer voice:** "Our automation breaks every time a process deviates from the standard path, and every exception forces us to build a workaround."

Same underlying need. Completely different orientation. The second version is the one a rep can open a conversation with. The first version is the one nobody uses.

Rewrite every pain point until it passes this test. If it sounds like a requirements document, it's not done.

## The Empathy Discipline

For technical thinkers, the failure isn't lack of empathy — it's that the instinct to organize and solve kicks in before listening is finished. The skill to develop:

- **Sit in the problem longer before reaching for the solution.** Most technical thinkers solve too fast.
- **Write pain points in the customer's voice** and read them back until they sound like a real person.
- **Ask "why does this matter to the person signing the check?" one more time than feels necessary.**

This doesn't come from a framework. It comes from customer interviews, customer calls, support tickets — and the discipline of not editing what you hear into product language before writing it down.

## Connection to the Challenger Sale

Problem-first messaging gives [[matthew-dixon-brent-adamson]] Challenger reps the raw material to teach and reframe:

- **Pain points** become the opening — the commercial insight that reframes how the customer thinks about their problem
- **The transition from pain points to features** becomes the reframe — teaching the customer something new about their situation
- **The value proposition** becomes the insight that only your solution can deliver

A capability-first document can't support Challenger selling. The rep has no problem-oriented opening to work from. They're left pitching features and hoping the buyer sees the connection.

## The Structural Consequence

Problem-first messaging is not just a tone choice — it's an architectural decision that flows through the entire [[master-messaging-document]]:

- Pillars are named for customer problem clusters, not product capabilities
- Pain points are the first element inside each pillar
- The value proposition is articulated as a business outcome (see [[benefit-ladder]]), not a feature benefit
