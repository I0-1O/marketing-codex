# Presentation as Story

## Definition

A presentation is a story told by a person. Slides are visual support for that story — not the story itself. The presenter carries the message. The slides help. When slides try to do the carrying, the message gets lighter, not heavier.

The test for any deck: can it be fully understood without a presenter? If yes, it is a document pretending to be a presentation. Build a document instead.

## Why It Matters

Most presentation failures trace back to a single misunderstanding: the belief that the deck is the presentation. That belief produces bullet walls, text overload, and slides that make the presenter redundant. Audiences disengage. Presenters read aloud. Nobody needed to be in the room.

The opposite — slides that require a presenter to work — creates attention, narrative pull, and the only thing a deck can never replicate: a person with conviction telling a story.

## The See-It vs. Hear-It Test

The diagnostic for every piece of text on a slide:

**Does the audience need to SEE this?**
Labels. Anchors. Statistics. Position statements. Diagrams showing relationships. Numbers that hit harder visually. Phrases that benefit from being read and heard simultaneously.

**Does the audience need to HEAR this?**
Explanations. Context. The story behind a number. Transitions. The "so what." Setup that makes the punchline land.

If the audience needs to hear it, it belongs in speaker notes. Not on the slide. Richard Mayer's multimedia learning research confirms this: narration + graphics outperforms narration + graphics + on-screen text. Adding the text hurts.

**What earns a place on the slide:**
A position statement that lands harder read and heard simultaneously. A closing argument. A line with rhetorical weight. These are intentional choices, not defaults.

- *On the slide:* "We didn't pivot to meet this moment. We were already here." ✓ (position statement)
- *On the slide:* "Some organizations operate under mandates that restrict where data can be processed or stored." ✗ (explanation — say it, show "Data Sovereignty")

## The Three Bad Habits

**1. Teleprompter slide** — presenter writes everything they plan to say onto the slide and reads it. Audience reads faster. Attention fractures immediately.

**2. Thoroughness-as-clarity** — presenter fears the audience will miss something, so the slide explains everything. Presenter becomes redundant. Audience wonders why they came.

**3. The leave-behind fallacy** — deck loaded with text to serve absent attendees. Constraint no longer applies in a world of recordings, transcripts, and AI summaries. See [[leave-behind-fallacy]].

## Speaker Notes Structure

The speaker notes are the presentation. The slides are the punctuation. Three-part structure for every slide:

**On This Slide** — strategic purpose. Why this slide exists at this point in the story. Not a content recap.

**What to Know** — everything the presenter should have in their head but doesn't necessarily say. Full explanations behind labels. Background context. Supporting data. Likely questions. The depth layer that makes the difference between handling follow-ups with confidence vs. "let me get back to you."

**What to Say** — the actual talk track. Verbatim for high-stakes moments. Loose talking points for conversational slides. Narrative direction throughout.

## Structural Rules

- **Many simple slides beat fewer complex ones.** Slides are free. Confused audiences are not.
- **One slide, one concept.** "And also" = next slide.
- **Vary visual weight.** Dense → simple → section break. Audiences need rhythm.
- **Bullets: 3 ideal, 5 ceiling.** Each bullet is a label. Presenter provides meaning. A bullet that fully explains itself has crossed to "hear it" territory.
- **Headlines state the point, not the topic.** "Integration" is a label. "Connect to Any System Without Code" is a headline.

## The Pyramid Principle: Think vs. Present

[[barbara-minto]]'s Pyramid Principle (lead with answer, support with arguments, back with evidence) is correct for documents and executive memos — where the reader controls pace and may stop at paragraph one.

Applied literally to a live presentation, it eliminates tension. Answer on slide one. Rest of deck is defense. No journey. No reason to stay engaged.

**The right use:** pyramid underneath a narrative arc. Use it to organize the logic. Use [[nancy-duarte]]'s what-is/what-could-be oscillation for the delivery. The pyramid ensures rigor. The story ensures the audience cares.

Use the pyramid to think. Use the story to present.

## The Build-Backwards Method

Most people build presentations wrong: open PowerPoint → create slides → fill with content → figure out what to say.

Reverse it:
1. Write the full talk track first. Story, arc, arguments, transitions. No slides.
2. Go back and ask: what visual support does each moment need?
3. Some moments need a stat. Some a diagram. Some a label. Some nothing — a blank screen with full audience attention is a tool.

[[garr-reynolds]] calls this analog planning: sketch on paper before touching software. Separates thinking from formatting. PowerPoint-first thinking produces bullet points. Paper-first thinking produces ideas.

## Case Study: Steve Jobs, iPhone 2007

Jobs' Macworld slides were almost absurdly simple — single image, a few words, a product name. 80 minutes of talk. Rarely more than one visual and a short headline per slide.

iPhone reveal: three icons (iPod, phone, internet communicator) — audience believed three products. Then the reveal: one device. The moment landed because the audience was listening to him, not reading ahead on a slide that gave away the punchline.

His slides never competed with him. Never made him redundant. Showed what words couldn't convey. His voice provided what slides should never attempt.
