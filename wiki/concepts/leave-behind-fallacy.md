# Leave-Behind Fallacy

## Definition

The belief that a slide deck must function as a standalone document for people who weren't in the room. Once a reasonable constraint. Now obsolete. Most decks are still built this way — loaded with text, explanations, and context that belong in a document — because the habit survived the technology shift that eliminated the underlying problem.

## Why It Matters

The leave-behind constraint is the primary driver of "slideuments" — decks that work perfectly without a presenter, which is another way of saying they don't need one. Every sentence added to serve an absent audience costs the live audience attention, clarity, and a reason to be present.

## How It Happened

**Pre-recording world:** Meetings happened in conference rooms. Miss the meeting, miss the content. The deck was the only artifact. It had to serve two jobs: visual aid for the room, standalone document for everyone else. Text got loaded in because it had to. Reasonable constraint. Predictable tradeoff.

**Virtual meetings:** Same problem persisted. Calendars overlapped. Time zones conflicted. The deck still had to work as a leave-behind. The habit survived the shift to video calls because the underlying constraint hadn't changed.

**Now:** Meetings can be recorded with one click. Full transcripts auto-generate. AI summarizes an hour-long discussion in seconds. The absent attendee can watch, read, or skim. The deck no longer needs to be the only record. The constraint is gone.

And yet most decks are still built as if it isn't.

## The Fix

If you need a standalone document, write a standalone document. Do the work. Make it good.

Don't compromise every slide in the deck to solve a problem that a recording and transcript already solve better.

If someone missed the meeting: send the recording. Send the transcript. Send the AI summary. Do not send a deck and tell them it explains everything — because the deck that explains everything is also the deck that made the live meeting worse.
