# Slides Don't Talk
by Brian Rieb

## Story Is the Job
Let me make a seemingly obvious statement:

"A presentation is a story told by a person to a room full of people."

Duh! That sentence should be straight forward. 

It 
is 
not. 

If it were obvious, most presentations (slide decks) would not look the way they do. They would not be walls of text projected onto a screen while a person stands next to them and reads aloud. They would not be documents pretending to be presentations. They would not be decks that work perfectly fine without anyone presenting them, which is another way of saying they do not need a presenter at all.

The job of a presentation is to tell a story. The slides help tell that story. They do not tell it for you. They do not narrate it. They do not replace you. They are a visual aid that supports the person in the room doing the actual work of communicating.

Think about the last fantasy novel you read. Somewhere in the front matter there was a map. A detailed rendering of the world. Cities, rivers, mountain ranges, political boundaries. That map existed so you could orient yourself while reading. You did not sit with the map open for the entire book. You did not flip to it every chapter. You maybe looked at it once, early on, to understand the geography. Then you read the story.

A detailed explainer slide is that map. It has its place. Maybe once. Maybe to orient the audience before the real content starts. But if every slide in your deck is a map, you are not telling a story. You are giving a geography lesson. And your audience checked out ten minutes ago.

Nothing should get between you, your story, and your audience.

## Where This Goes Wrong
I built a deck a while back called "Stop Building Ugly Slides." The subtitle was: "We've all sat through terrible slides. Here's how to stop making them." It's audience was mostly aimed at engineers and product people - although I believe the greatest offenders are those presentations at he executive level. 

The thesis was simple. The four most common slide offenses are bullet walls, text overload, the spreadsheet-on-a-slide, and the seven-concepts-crammed-onto-one-page slide. But those are symptoms. The real problem is three habits of thinking that produce them.

The first habit is using the slide as a teleprompter. The presenter writes down everything they plan to say, puts it on the slide, and reads it. The slide becomes a script. The audience reads faster than the presenter talks. Attention fractures.

The second habit is confusing thoroughness with clarity. The presenter worries the audience will miss something if the slide does not explain everything. So the slide explains everything. The presenter becomes redundant. The audience wonders why they are in a room when they could be reading a memo.

The third habit is the leave-behind fallacy. And this one deserves its own section because it has a history that explains why so many smart people still do it.

## The Leave-Behind Fallacy
For decades, building a deck that could stand alone was not a bad practice. It was a necessary one.

Meetings happened in conference rooms. If you were not in that conference room, you missed the meeting. There was no recording. There was no transcript. The only artifact was the deck. So the deck had to serve two jobs: it was a visual aid for the people in the room, and it was a standalone document for the people who were not.

This was a reasonable constraint. It produced a predictable outcome. Decks got loaded with text because they had to function as documents. The room suffered for it, but at least the people who missed the meeting could open the attachment and get the full picture.

Then meetings went virtual. But the same problem persisted. People still missed meetings. Calendars overlapped. Time zones conflicted. Someone was always absent. So the deck still had to work as a leave-behind. The habit survived the shift to video calls because the underlying constraint had not changed.

But that constraint has changed. Fundamentally.

Meetings can be recorded with a single click. Full transcripts are auto-generated. AI can summarize an hour-long discussion into a page of notes in seconds. The person who missed the meeting no longer needs the deck to reconstruct what happened. They can watch the recording. Read the transcript. Skim the summary. The deck no longer has to carry the weight of being the only record of the conversation.

And yet, most people still build decks as if it does.

Garr Reynolds identified this exact problem years ago. He coined the term "slideument" for the slide-document hybrid. In *Presentation Zen* (first published 2008, now in its third edition), he argued that merging slides and documents produces something that fails at both. His point was straightforward: slides are slides, documents are documents, and the attempt to combine them serves neither the room nor the reader. Reynolds drew on principles of simplicity and restraint from Japanese design to argue that the best slides need a presenter to work. That is a feature, not a flaw.

The leave-behind fallacy is not laziness. It is institutional memory from a world that no longer exists. If you need a standalone document, write a standalone document. Spend the time making it good. But do not compromise every slide in a deck to solve a problem that a recording and a transcript already solve better.

Edward Tufte made a related argument in *The Cognitive Style of PowerPoint* (2006). He showed that the default structures of slideware, particularly the bullet-list hierarchy, actively degrade the quality of serious analysis. His most devastating example was from the Columbia shuttle investigation, where engineers communicated critical safety data through nested PowerPoint bullets that obscured the severity of what they were reporting. The format flattened the information. The stakes were life and death.

Tufte's critique was aimed at the tool itself. But the deeper problem is not PowerPoint. It is the belief that the slides carry the message. They never have. The presenter carries the message. The slides help. When the slides try to do the carrying, the message gets lighter, not heavier.

## See It or Hear It
Some things the audience needs to **see**. Labels. Anchors. Statistics. Position statements. A diagram that shows a relationship. A number that hits harder as a visual than spoken aloud. A phrase that benefits from being read and heard at the same time.

Some things the audience needs to **hear**. Explanations. Context. The story behind the number. The transition between ideas. The "so what" that connects a feature to a business outcome. The setup that makes the punchline land.

When you put things the audience needs to hear onto the slide, you create competition. The audience reads and listens simultaneously, doing neither well. Richard Mayer's research on multimedia learning found that people learn better from narration paired with graphics than from narration paired with graphics *and* on-screen text. Adding the text does not help. It hurts.

The test is simple. Look at every piece of text on a slide and ask: if I removed this and just said it, would the slide still work? If yes, the text belongs in your notes. Not on the slide.

Sentences can earn their place on a slide. But they earn it by doing something a label or bullet cannot do. A position statement. A closing argument. A line that carries rhetorical weight and benefits from being read and heard simultaneously. These are intentional choices. Not the default.

Here is the difference.

On the slide: *"We didn't pivot to meet this moment. We were already here."*

That works. It is a position statement. It lands harder when the audience reads it while the presenter delivers it. Remove it from the slide and just say it and you lose something.

Now compare: *"Some organizations operate under mandates that restrict where data can be processed or stored."*

That is an explanation. It is setup. It is what you say while the slide shows a label that reads "Data Sovereignty" and maybe a map. Putting that sentence on the slide turns the presenter into a narrator of their own content.

## Speaker Notes Are the Presentation
If the slides are not the presentation, where does the presentation live?

In the speaker notes.

I use a three-part structure for every slide:

**On This Slide.** What is here and why. Not a content recap. The strategic purpose. Why this slide exists at this point in the story.

**What to Know.** Everything the presenter should have in their head but does not necessarily need to say. The full explanation behind each label. Background context. Supporting data. Likely questions. Technical details that matter only if asked. This is the depth layer. The difference between a presenter who handles follow-ups with confidence and one who says "let me get back to you on that."

**What to Say.** The actual talk track. Verbatim language for high-stakes moments. Loose talking points for conversational slides. Narrative arc direction for everything in between. The format varies. The purpose does not: this section tells the presenter what story to tell and how to tell it.

This is where Nancy Duarte's work in *Resonate* (2010) matters. Her central insight was the contrast between "what is" and "what could be." The best presentations oscillate between the current reality and a better future, building tension and resolution until the audience is ready to act. The speaker creates that movement. Not the slides.

Duarte also reframed the roles. The audience is the hero. The presenter is the mentor. Your job is not to demonstrate how smart you are. Your job is to give the audience the tools and motivation to change something. That framing lives in the speaker notes. In what you say and how you say it and the arc you build across the talk. Not in bullet points on a projected screen.

## Structural Principles
A few rules that hold up across every deck I have built or reviewed.

**Many simple slides beat fewer complex ones.** A single statistic on a slide with nothing else can be the most powerful moment in a presentation. The impulse to consolidate information onto fewer slides is almost always wrong. The cost of an additional slide is zero. The cost of a cluttered slide is lost attention. Slides are free. Confused audiences are not.

**One slide, one concept.** If you find yourself saying "and also" while on a slide, that is your next slide. When a slide tries to cover multiple ideas, the presenter is the only person who knows how they connect. The audience is just lost.

**Vary the visual weight.** Two dense slides back-to-back create fatigue. Follow a complex diagram with a simple stat. Follow a content-heavy slide with a section break. The audience needs rhythm. Not every scene is a chase sequence.

**Three bullets is ideal. Five is the ceiling.** Each bullet is a label or short phrase. The presenter provides the meaning. If a bullet is a complete explanation, it has crossed the line from what the audience needs to see to what the audience needs to hear.

**Headlines state the point. They do not label the topic.** "Integration" is a label. "Connect to Any System Without Code" is a headline. The headline tells the audience where you stand. The label tells them what room they are in.

## The Pyramid Principle: Use It to Think, Not to Present
Barbara Minto developed the Pyramid Principle at McKinsey in the 1960s. It remains the standard framework for structured business communication at major consulting firms. Lead with the answer. Support it with key arguments. Back each argument with evidence. Top-down. Conclusion first.

This is excellent advice for documents. For written reports. For executive memos. For any situation where the reader controls the pace and might stop reading after the first paragraph.

But apply it literally to a live presentation and you create a specific problem. If you put the answer on slide one and spend the rest of the deck defending it, you have eliminated tension. You have given away the ending. The audience knows where you are going. They have no reason to stay engaged because there is no journey. There is a conclusion followed by evidence.

The best presenters use the pyramid underneath a narrative arc. The recommendation exists. The pyramid organizes the logic. But the delivery follows a different shape. Duarte's oscillation between current state and future state. A problem, then a possibility. Tension, then resolution. The pyramid ensures the argument is rigorous. The story ensures the audience cares.

Use the pyramid to think. Use the story to present.

## Jobs Understood This
When Steve Jobs introduced the iPhone at Macworld in January 2007, his slides were almost absurdly simple. A single image. A few words. A product name.

Jobs talked for roughly 80 minutes. His slides rarely contained more than a single visual and a short headline. When he introduced the concept of the iPhone, the slide showed three icons: an iPod, a phone, and an internet communicator. He let the audience believe these were three separate products. Then he revealed they were one device.

The moment landed because the audience was listening to him. They were not reading ahead on a slide that gave away the punchline. He was telling a story. The slides were supporting it. The story belonged to him.

His slides never competed with him. They never made him redundant. They never contained an explanation the audience could read faster than he could deliver it. They showed what words could not convey. His voice provided what slides should not attempt.

## The Practical Shift
Most people build presentations the wrong way around. They open PowerPoint first. They create slides. They fill those slides with content. Then they figure out what to say about each slide.

Reverse it.

Start by writing what you want to say. The full talk track. The story. The arc. The arguments. The transitions. Write it without a single slide in front of you.

Then go back and decide: what visual support does each moment in this talk need?

Some moments need a stat on screen. Some need a diagram. Some need a photo. Some need a label that anchors the topic while you provide the depth. Some need nothing at all. Some of the most powerful moments in a presentation happen with a blank screen. The audience has nothing to look at except you. Their full attention is on what you are saying. That is a tool.

Garr Reynolds recommends starting with paper and pen, sketching ideas before touching software. He calls it analog planning. The point is to separate the thinking from the formatting. When you start in PowerPoint, the tool shapes your thinking. You think in slides. You think in bullet points. You think in templates. When you start on paper, you think in ideas. You think in story.

## The Bottom Line
Slides do not talk. You talk. The slides are there to help you tell a story.

Every piece of text on a slide should pass a single test: does the audience need to see this, or do I need to say it? If you need to say it, it belongs in your notes. If the audience needs to see it, it belongs on the slide.

Fill your speaker notes. They are not an afterthought. They are the presentation. The slides are the punctuation.

If you build a deck that makes perfect sense without a presenter, you have built a document. Call it what it is. Print it. Email it. Do not stand in front of a room and narrate it.

Build presentations that need you. Build presentations that tell a story only you can tell. That is the whole point.
